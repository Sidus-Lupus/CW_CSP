What's a Wolf?

What do you picture when you think of a wolf? Probably a gray wolf or timber wolf. What about a maned wof? Interestingly, depending on how you catagorize "wolf" status, the Maned Wolf isn't a wolf. For the purposes of this webpage "wolf" will solely refer to members of the genus Canis. Furthermore, I will specifiy what species I'm talking about, for example I would specifically mention red wolves when talking about red wolves. Gray Wolf

Let's start with Gray wolves, also known as Canis Lupus. These are your standard wolf: large build, wide face, trianglar ears, bottlebrush tail with a black spot. Here's a fun fact: there's 37 different subspecies of gray wolf! I won't be touching on subspecies, but they're worth a google if you're interested. For example, different subspecies live in different parts of the world.
As I just mentioned, Gray wolves can be found everywhere from Asia to North America. However, they're not present in Africa, South America, or similarily southern locations. Fun fact: if you have a pet dog or met one, that pupper is descended from the ancestors of wild gray wolves. They just look pretty different because of domestication and selective breeding. Gray wolves also live in packs that can range from 4-10 members. All the packmates work together to hunt and live, and will often play with eachother. Wikipedia link for further reading: Gray Wolf.

Speaking of wolf packs...

Alphas? Nah. Parents? Yeah.

Misconception number 1: Wolf packs are structured with alphas, betas, omegas, and other groupings. Misconception number 2: Wolf packs are structured based on domincance.
Let's start by going over these misconceptions. The idea that wolf packs are structured based on domincance comes from a now debunked scientific observation of wolves in captivity. These were wolves that had never met each other and were forced to live and work togeter. To form some kind of order, it is true that these wolves resorted to displays of domincance. Imagine you and 19 other random people were put in charge of a hotel. Of course someone's going to use intimidation and charisma to take charge of the whole group. However, it is important to note that this was in captivity. In the wild, wolf packs are just families. These wild packs are contstructed of a breeding pair, mom and dad, and all of their offspring. They all hunt together, play together, live togther. An honestly cute example of how members of a wild wolf pack work together is pupsitting: where one pack member will watch and protect the wolf pups while the other members go hunting.
